The Paradox (Eubulides of Miletus, ~4th century BC):

The Sorites Paradox (from Greek σωρός, "heap") — also called the paradox of the heap. Eubulides asked: if you have a heap of sand and remove one grain, do you still have a heap? Applying this recursively, you could conclude that a single grain of sand is a heap. Or that any large collection of grains is not a heap. Both conclusions are absurd. The paradox has not been resolved in 2,400 years. Proposed solutions include fuzzy logic, supervaluationism, degree theory, and epistemicism. None are fully satisfying.

What it reveals:

Some predicates — "heap," "tall," "bald," "red" — resist precise definition. Language has vague predicates. Vague predicates have tolerance: applying them is unaffected by small changes. This tolerance, combined with large accumulated changes, produces the paradox. The problem is not with heaps. The problem is with the word "heap."

Zeno's Paradox
Achilles gives the tortoise a 100m head start.
To overtake it, he must first reach where it was.
By then it has moved. He must reach that new position.
By then it has moved again.
Infinite steps. He never catches up.
Except he does.

CONVERGENCE — INFINITE TERMS, FINITE SUM

The Paradox (Zeno of Elea, ~450 BC):

Achilles runs at 10 m/s. The tortoise at 1 m/s. The tortoise starts 100m ahead. To pass the tortoise, Achilles must first reach the tortoise's starting point. He covers 100m. The tortoise has moved 10m. He must cover 10m. The tortoise has moved 1m. He must cover 1m. The tortoise has moved 0.1m. This continues forever — an infinite sequence of tasks. Zeno argued this proves motion is impossible.

The Resolution (calculus, ~1666 AD):

100 + 10 + 1 + 0.1 + 0.01 + … = 111.11… = 111⅑ meters. A convergent series. An infinite number of terms summing to a finite value. Achilles overtakes the tortoise at t = 11.11s. The paradox reveals something true about infinity — it does not reveal something true about running.
An infinite number of steps can take a finite amount of time.
This was not obvious.
It required calculus to prove.
The paradox is resolved.
The unease it produces is not.

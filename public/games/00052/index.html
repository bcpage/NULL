The Illegal Prime

1,401-digit prime number — computed 2001
Discovered byPhil Carmody
DateJanuary 2001
Digits1,401
ClassificationPrime (verified)
Legal statusDisputed (US, 2001–2003)

In January 2001, mathematician Phil Carmody discovered that a specific 1,401-digit prime number — when written in hexadecimal, treated as a gzip-compressed binary, and decompressed — produces the complete source code for DeCSS: the program that decrypts DVD Content Scramble System (CSS) encryption.

The Motion Picture Association of America had obtained federal injunctions against distribution of DeCSS under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act. Carmody constructed his prime deliberately: he took the compressed DeCSS binary, padded it to make it represent a large integer, then searched the surrounding neighborhood for the nearest prime. The 1,401-digit result was the smallest prime he found.

This created a paradox. The MPAA's legal position implied that certain integers were contraband — that the act of transmitting a specific sequence of digits constituted a federal offense under 17 U.S.C. § 1201. Carmody's prime was posted to Slashdot on January 15, 2001. It appeared on T-shirts, in academic papers, carved into bar soap, and printed in newspapers. The number spread as an act of civil disobedience.

The question was never definitively adjudicated: can a number be illegal? The injunctions targeted specific software, not the mathematics underlying it. No one was ever prosecuted for possessing or distributing the prime itself.

The number above is a prime. It contains no instructions. It is a fact about integers that has always been true and will always be true. Whether it is legal to read it is a question the courts declined to answer.
